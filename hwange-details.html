Hwange National Park

Matabeleland North, Zimbabwe

About Hwange National Park

Hwange National Park (formerly Wankie Game Reserve) is Zimbabwe's largest natural reserve, covering approximately 14,650 square kilometers. Established in 1928, it's one of Africa's premier wildlife destinations, renowned for its tremendous elephant population and diverse ecosystems ranging from desert sands to sparse woodland.

Wildlife

Hwange boasts one of Africa's largest elephant populations (approximately 45,000) and is home to all of Zimbabwe's specially protected species: the elephant, lion, wild dog, buffalo, leopard, and rhino. The park supports over 100 mammal species including 19 large herbivores and 8 large carnivores. Notable species include the endangered African wild dog, cheetah, and both black and white rhino.

Unique Features

Hwange's landscape varies from semi-desert scrubland in the southwest to open grasslands and woodlands in the north. The park has no major rivers and relies on over 60 pumped waterholes to sustain wildlife during the dry season. These waterholes become wildlife gathering points, offering spectacular game viewing opportunities.

Conservation

Hwange plays a crucial role in Zimbabwe's conservation efforts. The park faces challenges from poaching (particularly elephants), human-wildlife conflict, and climate change. Conservation programs focus on anti-poaching, community engagement, and wildlife research. The park is part of the Kavango-Zambezi Transfrontier Conservation Area (KAZA TFCA), the world's largest transfrontier conservation area.

Tourism

Hwange offers diverse safari experiences including game drives, walking safaris, and night drives. The park has several hides overlooking waterholes where visitors can observe wildlife at close range. Accommodation ranges from luxury lodges to basic campsites. The best time to visit is during the dry season (May to October) when animals congregate around water sources.
