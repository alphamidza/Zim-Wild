Matopos National Park

Matabeleland South, Zimbabwe

About Matopos National Park

Matopos National Park, also known as Matobo National Park, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in southern Zimbabwe. The park covers 424 square kilometers of unique granite kopjes (rock formations) and wooded valleys. The name "Matobo" means "bald heads" in the local Ndebele language, referring to the distinctive rounded granite hills.

Wildlife

The park is home to approximately 300 rhinos (both black and white), making it one of the best places in Africa to see these endangered species. Other wildlife includes leopards, sable antelope, and over 175 bird species. The park's rhino population is particularly significant, with successful conservation programs that allow visitors to track rhinos on foot with experienced guides.

Geological Features

Matopos is famous for its dramatic balancing rock formations created by millions of years of erosion. The park contains some of the most spectacular granite scenery in the world, with whaleback domes and castle kopjes. These formations have created numerous caves that hold significant archaeological and historical importance.

Cultural Significance

The park contains over 3,000 registered rock art sites, with some paintings dating back 13,000 years. These were created by the San people and are among the best preserved in southern Africa. The park is also the burial place of Cecil John Rhodes at World's View, and has significant Ndebele cultural heritage.

Conservation

Matopos is a stronghold for rhino conservation in Zimbabwe, with intensive anti-poaching efforts. The park's varied habitats support diverse flora including over 200 tree species and numerous aloes. The Zimbabwean government and private organizations work together to protect this unique ecosystem.

Tourism

Visitors can enjoy rhino tracking on foot, game drives, and tours of the rock art sites. The park offers stunning viewpoints, particularly at World's View, and has several lodges and campsites. The best time to visit is during the dry season (May to October) when wildlife viewing is at its best.
